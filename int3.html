How can I evaluate $$ \int_{0}^{\infty}\frac{\ln\left(a^2x^2+1\right)}{b^2x^2+1} \, \mathrm dx $$ for $a \geq 0$ and $b>0$?

Fix $b>0$ and consider the integral parameterized by $a \geq 0$: $$\displaystyle I(a) = \int_{0}^{\infty}\frac{\ln\left(a^2x^2+1\right)}{b^2x^2+1} \, \mathrm dx. $$ Differentiating both sides with respect to $a$, applying partial fractions, and evaluating the integral gives $$\begin{aligned} I'(a) &= 2a\int_{0}^{\infty}\frac{x^2}{\left(a^2x^2+1\right)\left(b^2x^2+1\right)} \, \mathrm dx \\ &= \frac{2a}{a^2-b^2}\int_{0}^{\infty}\left(\frac{1}{b^2x^2+1} - \frac{1}{a^2x^2+1}\right) \, \mathrm dx \\ &= \frac{2a}{a^2-b^2}\left(\frac{1}{b}\arctan(bx) - \frac{1}{a}\arctan(ax)\right)\Bigg|_{x=0}^{x\to\infty} \\ &= \frac{2a}{a^2-b^2}\cdot \frac{\pi}{2}\left(\frac{1}{b}-\frac{1}{a}\right) \\ &= \frac{\pi}{b(a+b)}. \end{aligned} $$ Now, we integrate both sides of the above from $0$ to $a$: $$\begin{aligned} I(a) - I(0) &= \frac{\pi}{b}\int_{0}^{a}\frac{1}{t+b} \, \mathrm dt \\ &= \frac{\pi}{b}\ln(t+b)\Bigg|_{t=0}^{t=a} \\ &= \frac{\pi}{b}\ln\left(\frac{a+b}{b}\right). \end{aligned} $$ Since $I(0)=0$, we conclude that $$ \int_{0}^{\infty}\frac{\ln\left(a^2x^2+1\right)}{b^2x^2+1} \, \mathrm dx = \frac{\pi}{b}\ln\left(\frac{a+b}{b}\right). $$
