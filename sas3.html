What is the value of $$ \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{4^k(k!)^2}{(2k+1)^2(2k)!}? $$

We want to evaluate the infinite series $$ S = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{4^k(k!)^2}{(2k+1)^2(2k)!}. $$ Before we begin, I would like to say that I arrived at the following solution by making a *mistake* in a recent problem I was working on. :)

We start from afar with $$ I = \int_{0}^{1}\frac{\ln\left(\frac{1+x}{1-x}\right)}{\sqrt{1-x^2}} \, \mathrm dx. $$ Now, we “naturally” break up the integral: $$ I = \int_{0}^{1}\left(\ln(1+x) - \ln(1-x)\right) \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}} \, \mathrm dx, $$ then substitute the Maclaurin series for the logs and rearrange: $$\begin{aligned} I &= \int_{0}^{1}\left(\left(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n}x^n\right) - \left(-\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n}x^n\right)\right) \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}} \, \mathrm dx \\ &= \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^{n+1}+1}{n}\int_{0}^{1}\frac{x^{n}}{\sqrt{1-x^2}} \, \mathrm dx. \end{aligned} $$ Since $(-1)^{n+1}+1 = 2$ for odd $n$ and $(-1)^{n+1}+1 = 0$ for even $n$ we have via $n = 2k+1$: $$ I = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{2}{2k+1}\int_{0}^{1}\frac{x^{2k+1}}{\sqrt{1-x^2}} \, \mathrm dx. $$ We handle the inner integral via the substitution $t = x^2$ and discover the beta function hiding out: $$\begin{aligned} I &= \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{1}{2k+1}\int_{0}^{1}\frac{t^k}{\sqrt{1-t}} \, \mathrm d t \\ &= \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{1}{2k+1}\mathrm{B}(k+1,1/2) \\ &= \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{1}{2k+1}\cdot\frac{\Gamma(k+1)\Gamma(1/2)}{\Gamma(k+3/2)}. \end{aligned} $$ However, noting that $\Gamma(1/2)=\sqrt{\pi}$, that $k$ is a non-negative integer, and that the gamma function satisfies the recurrence $\Gamma(x+1)=x\Gamma(x)$ we can greatly simplify $I$ and ultimately relate it to $S$: $$\begin{aligned} I &= 2\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{4^k(k!)^2}{(2k+1)^2(2k)!} \\ &= 2S. \end{aligned} $$ Great. Now what? We again start with $$ I = \int_{0}^{1}\frac{\ln\left(\frac{1+x}{1-x}\right)}{\sqrt{1-x^2}} \, \mathrm dx, $$ but this time we take a different path through the mathematical forest. First, we substitute $u = \frac{1+x}{1-x}$: $$\begin{aligned} I &= \int_{1}^{\infty}\frac{\ln(u)}{\sqrt{\left(1-\frac{u-1}{u+1}\right)\left(1+\frac{u-1}{u+1}\right)}}\cdot\frac{2}{(u+1)^2} \, \mathrm du \\ &= \int_{1}^{\infty}\frac{\ln(u)}{\sqrt{u}(u+1)} \, \mathrm du, \end{aligned} $$ then $u=\frac{1}{s^2}$: $$\begin{aligned} I &= \int_{1}^{0} \frac{\ln\left(\frac{1}{s^2}\right)}{\left(\frac{1}{s}\right)\left(\frac{1}{s^2}+1\right)} \cdot -\frac{2}{s^3} \, \mathrm ds \\ &= -4\int_{0}^{1}\frac{\ln(s)}{s^2+1} \, \mathrm ds \\ &= 4G, \end{aligned} $$ where $G$ is Catalan’s constant coming from its most well known integral identity.

Therefore, by following $2$ different paths through the mathematical forest we have found both that $$ I = 2S = 4G, $$ and so we solve for $S$ to conclude that $$ S = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{4^k(k!)^2}{(2k+1)^2(2k)!} = 2G. $$
