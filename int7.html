What is the value of $$ \int_{0}^{\pi/2}\ln \sin x \, \mathrm dx? $$

We want to evaluate the integral $$ J = \int_{0}^{\pi/2}\ln \sin x \, \mathrm dx. $$ Here is a nifty approach that uses differentiation under the integral sign.

First we integrate by parts with $u=\ln \sin x$ and $\mathrm dv = \mathrm dx$: $$ J = x\ln \sin x \Bigg|_{0}^{\pi/2} - \int_{0}^{\pi/2}x\cot x \, \mathrm dx = - \int_{0}^{\pi/2}\frac{x}{\tan x} \, \mathrm dx, $$ then we let $t=\tan x$ or equivalently $x=\arctan t$: $$ J = -\int_{0}^{\infty}\frac{\arctan t}{t\left(t^2+1\right)} \, \mathrm dt. $$ Now, consider the related integral parameterized by $a\geq 0$: $$ I(a) = \int_{0}^{\infty}\frac{\arctan at}{t\left(t^2+1\right)} \, \mathrm dt, $$ where differentiating under the integral sign with respect to $a$ yields $$\begin{aligned} I'(a) &= \int_{0}^{\infty}\frac{\partial}{\partial a}\frac{\arctan at}{t\left(t^2+1\right)} \, \mathrm dt \\ &= \int_{0}^{\infty}\frac{1}{\left(a^2t^2+1\right)\left(t^2+1\right)} \, \mathrm dt. \end{aligned} $$ By partial fractions, we obtain easily evaluable integrals: $$\begin{aligned} I'(a) &= \frac{1}{a^2-1}\int_{0}^{\infty}\left(\frac{a^2}{a^2t^2+1} - \frac{1}{t^2+1} \right) \, \mathrm dt \\ &= \frac{1}{a^2-1}\left(\frac{\pi a}{2} - \frac{\pi}{2}\right) \\ &= \frac{\pi}{2}\cdot \frac{1}{a+1}. \end{aligned} $$ Further integrating $I'(a)$ with respect to $a$ easily yields $$ I(a) = \frac{\pi}{2}\int\frac{1}{a+1} \, \mathrm da = \frac{\pi}{2}\ln(a+1) + C, $$ where the constant $C = 0$ is recovered by noting that $I(0) = 0$. Finally, $J = -I(1) = -\frac{\pi}{2}\ln 2$ and so we conclude that $$ \int_{0}^{\pi/2}\ln \sin x \, \mathrm dx = -\frac{\pi}{2}\ln 2. $$
