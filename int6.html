What is the value of $$ \int_{0}^{\infty}x^n\sin\left(x^k\right) \, \mathrm dx? $$

$$\textbf{Case 1: } k>0 \tag*{}$$ Fixing $(k>1+n) \land (k>-1-n)$ (for convergence), consider parameterizing the integral by $t>0$ as $$ I(t) = \int_{0}^{\infty}x^n\sin\left(tx^k\right) \, \mathrm dx. $$ Taking the Laplace transform of both sides yields (interchanging the order of integration by dominated convergence) $$\begin{aligned} \mathcal{L}\left[I(t)\right] &= \int_{0}^{\infty}x^n\int_{0}^{\infty}\sin\left(tx^k\right)e^{-st} \, \mathrm dt \, \mathrm dx \\ &= \int_{0}^{\infty} \frac{x^{n+k}}{x^{2k}+s^2} \, \mathrm dx. \end{aligned} $$ For symmetry purposes let $s^2 = b^{2k}$ then apply the transformation $x=b/w$: $$ \mathcal{L}\left[I(t)\right] = \int_{0}^{\infty} \frac{x^{n+k}}{x^{2k}+b^{2k}} \, \mathrm dx = \frac{1}{b^{k-n-1}}\int_{0}^{\infty}\frac{w^{k-n-2}}{w^{2k}+1} \, \mathrm dw, $$ where we restore $s$ then take the inverse Laplace transform of both sides: $$\begin{aligned} I(t) &= \mathcal{L}^{-1}\left[\frac{1}{s^{1-n/k-1/k}}\right]\int_{0}^{\infty}\frac{w^{k-n-2}}{w^{2k}+1} \, \mathrm dw \\ &= \frac{t^{-n/k-1/k}}{\Gamma(1-n/k-1/k)}\underbrace{\int_{0}^{\infty}\frac{w^{k-n-2}}{w^{2k}+1} \, \mathrm dw}_{J}. \end{aligned} $$ It remains to evaluate $J$. Break it into $2$ intervals, multiply the latter integral by $w^{âˆ’2k}$, then apply the geometric series to both integrals and carefully evaluate: $$\begin{aligned} J &= \int_{0}^{1}\frac{w^{k-n-2}}{w^{2k}+1} \, \mathrm dw + \int_{1}^{\infty}\frac{w^{-k-n-2}}{w^{-2k}+1} \, \mathrm dw \\ &= \sum_{m=0}^{\infty}(-1)^m\left(\int_{0}^{1}w^{2km+k-n-2} \, \mathrm dw + \int_{1}^{\infty}w^{-2km-k-n-2} \, \mathrm dw\right) \\ &= \sum_{m=0}^{\infty}(-1)^m\left(\frac{1}{2km+k-n-1} + \frac{1}{2km+k+n+1}\right). \end{aligned} $$ We now evoke the following series representation for $\sec z$ which can be found in a slightly different form here : $$ \sec z = \sum_{m=0}^{\infty}(-1)^m\left(\frac{1}{\pi m + \pi /2-z} + \frac{1}{\pi m + \pi /2+z}\right), \, \, \frac{z}{\pi}-\frac{1}{2} \notin \mathbb{Z}. $$ Letting $z=\frac{\pi(n+1)}{2k}$ and simplifying gives $$ \sec \frac{\pi(n+1)}{2k} = \frac{2k}{\pi}\sum_{m=0}^{\infty}(-1)^m\left(\frac{1}{2km+k-n-1} + \frac{1}{2km+k+n+1}\right). $$ Finally, we multiply both sides by $\frac{\pi}{2k}$: $$\begin{aligned} \frac{\pi}{2k} \sec \frac{\pi(n+1)}{2k} &= \sum_{m=0}^{\infty}(-1)^m\left(\frac{1}{2km+k-n-1} + \frac{1}{2km+k+n+1}\right) \\ &= J. \end{aligned} $$ Therefore, $$\begin{aligned} I(1) = \frac{1}{\Gamma(1-n/k-1/k)} \cdot \frac{\pi}{2k} \sec \frac{\pi(n+1)}{2k}. \end{aligned} $$ $$\textbf{Case 2: } k < 0 \tag*{}$$ Now, if $k < 0$, we replace $x$ with $x^{-1}$ in $I(t)$: $$ I(t) = \int_{0}^{\infty}x^{-n-2}\sin\left(tx^{-k}\right) \, \mathrm dx, $$ or in terms of $a=-k$ and $b=-n-2$: $$ I(t) = \int_{0}^{\infty}x^{b}\sin\left(tx^{a}\right) \, \mathrm dx, $$ we have already solved this problem in case $1$. From which we see that we must have $(k < 1+n) \land (k < -1-n)$ for convergence and our solution becomes $$\displaystyle I(1) = -\frac{1}{\Gamma(1-n/k-1/k)} \cdot \frac{\pi}{2k} \sec \frac{\pi(n+1)}{2k}. $$ In all, we conclude that: $$ \int_{0}^{\infty}x^n\sin\left(x^k\right) \, \mathrm dx = \frac{1}{\Gamma(1-n/k-1/k)} \cdot \frac{\pi}{2|k|} \sec \frac{\pi(n+1)}{2k}. $$
