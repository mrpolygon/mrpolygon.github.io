What is the value of $$ \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{k(3k+1)}? $$

We want to evaluate the infinite sum $$ \mathcal{I} = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{k(3k+1)}. $$ First, we relate the value of the sum in question to a dummy value $\mathcal{J}$: $$ \mathcal{J} = 1-\mathcal{I}, $$ where our goal will be to first find $\mathcal{J}$. Since by the well known telescoping series: $$ \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k(k+1)} = 1, $$ we may unconventionally rewrite $\mathcal{J}$ as $$ \mathcal{J} = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\left(\frac{1}{k(k+1)} - \frac{1}{k(3k+1)}\right), $$ then incorporate an integral into the sum: $$ \mathcal{J} = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\int_{0}^{1}\left(\frac{x^k}{k} - \frac{x^{3k}}{k}\right)\,\mathrm dx. $$ We now interchange the order of the sum and integral: $$ \mathcal{J} = \int_{0}^{1}\left(\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\frac{x^k}{k}-\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\frac{x^{3k}}{k}\right)\,\mathrm dx, $$ and notice that both sums are in the form of the Maclaurin series for the natural log. Therefore, we may write $$ \mathcal{J} = \int_{0}^{1}\left(\ln\left(1-x^3\right)-\ln(1-x)\right)\,\mathrm dx, $$ then combine the natural logs as $$ \mathcal{J} = \int_{0}^{1}\ln\left(\frac{1-x^3}{1-x}\right)\,\mathrm dx, $$ and since $$ 1+x+x^2 = \frac{1-x^3}{1-x}, $$ applying this gives $$ \mathcal{J} = \int_{0}^{1}\ln\left(1+x+x^2\right)\,\mathrm dx. $$ It remains to evaluate the lone integral. First, we apply integration by parts with $u=\ln(1+x+x^2)$ and $\mathrm dv = \mathrm dx$. This gives $$ \mathcal{J} = \underbrace{x\ln\left(1+x+x^2\right)\Bigg|_{0}^{1}}_{=\ln 3} - \int_{0}^{1}\frac{x+2x^2}{1+x+x^2}\, \mathrm dx. $$ We proceed by performing long division: $$ \int_{0}^{1}\frac{x+2x^2}{1+x+x^2}\, \mathrm dx = \underbrace{\int_{0}^{1}2\,\mathrm dx}_{=2} - \int_{0}^{1}\frac{2+x}{1+x+x^2}\,\mathrm dx, $$ and then rewrite: $$ \int_{0}^{1}\frac{2+x}{1+x+x^2}\,\mathrm dx = \frac{1}{2}\int_{0}^{1}\frac{1+2x}{1+x+x^2}\,\mathrm dx + \frac{3}{2}\int_{0}^{1}\frac{1}{1+x+x^2} \, \mathrm dx. $$ For the first integral, let $t=1+x+x^2$ to easily give $$ \int_{0}^{1}\frac{1+2x}{1+x+x^2}\,\mathrm dx = \int_{1}^{3}\frac{1}{t}\,\mathrm dt = \ln(t)\Bigg|_{1}^{3} = \ln 3. $$ For the latter integral, we complete the square then let $t=x+\frac{1}{2}$: $$ \begin{aligned} \int_{0}^{1}\frac{1}{1+x+x^2} \, \mathrm dx &= \int_{0}^{1}\frac{1}{\frac{3}{4}+\left(x+\frac{1}{2}\right)^2} \, \mathrm dx \\ &= \int_{1/2}^{3/2}\frac{1}{\frac{3}{4}+t^2} \, \mathrm dt \\ &= \frac{2}{\sqrt{3}}\arctan\left(\frac{2t}{\sqrt{3}}\right)\Bigg|_{1/2}^{3/2} \\ &= \frac{\pi}{3\sqrt{3}} \end{aligned} $$ Putting it altogether yields $$ \mathcal{J} = \frac{3}{2}\ln 3 + \frac{\pi}{2\sqrt{3}} - 2. $$ Finally, we conclude that $$ \mathcal{I} = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{k(3k+1)} = 1-\mathcal{J} = 3 - \frac{3}{2}\ln 3 - \frac{\pi}{2\sqrt{3}}. $$
