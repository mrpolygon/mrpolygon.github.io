What is the value of $$ I_{n} = \int_{0}^{2\pi}\ln(1+\cos x)\cos(nx) \, \mathrm dx $$ for $n = 1,2,3\ldots$?

For $n = 1,2,3\ldots$, we want to evaluate the integral $$I_{n} = \int_{0}^{2\pi}\ln(1+\cos x)\cos(nx) \, \mathrm dx.$$ We will initially apply integration by parts with $u=\ln(1+\cos x)$ to neutralize the logarithm: $$ I_{n} = \frac{1}{n}\int_{0}^{2\pi}\frac{\sin x\sin(nx)}{1+\cos x} \, \mathrm dx = \frac{1}{n}\int_{0}^{2\pi}\tan\left(\frac{x}{2}\right)\sin(nx) \, \mathrm dx. $$ Now, note that $$ \tan z = -i + \frac{2i}{1+e^{2iz}}, $$ and so letting $z=x/2$ in the above gives $$\begin{aligned} I_{n} &= \frac{1}{n}\int_{0}^{2\pi}\left(-i + \frac{2i}{1+e^{ix}}\right)\sin(nx) \, \mathrm dx \\ &= \frac{1}{n}\left[-i\int_{0}^{2\pi}\sin(nx) \, \mathrm dx + 2i\int_{0}^{2\pi}\frac{\sin(nx)}{1+e^{ix}} \, \mathrm dx\right]. \end{aligned}$$ However, by orthogonality, $$ \int_{0}^{2\pi}\sin(nx) \, \mathrm dx = 0 $$ We carefully apply the geometric series to the remaining integral: $$\begin{aligned} \int_{0}^{2\pi}\frac{\sin(nx)}{1+e^{ix}} \, \mathrm dx &= \int_{0}^{2\pi}\sin(nx)\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}(-1)^ke^{ikx} \, \mathrm dx \\ &= \sum_{\substack{k=0 \\ k \neq n}}^{\infty}(-1)^k\int_{0}^{2\pi}\sin(nx)e^{ikx} \, \mathrm dx + (-1)^n\int_{0}^{2\pi}\sin(nx)e^{inx} \, \mathrm dx . \end{aligned}$$ However, again by orthogonality, $$ \sum_{\substack{k=0 \\ k \neq n}}^{\infty}(-1)^k\int_{0}^{2\pi}\sin(nx)e^{ikx} \, \mathrm dx = 0 $$ and $$ \int_{0}^{2\pi}\sin(nx)e^{inx} \, \mathrm dx = i\pi. $$ Putting this all together, we conclude that $$ I_{n} = (-1)^{n+1}\frac{2\pi}{n}. $$
