What is the value of $$ \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{k^2}? $$

Here is an accessible proof sketch to the so-called Basel problem: $$S = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{k^2}. $$ We begin by defining a “generating” integral: $$ I = \int_{0}^{\pi/4}x(\tan x + \cot x) \, \mathrm dx. $$ Noting the complex definitions for the trigonometric terms and algebraically rearranging: $$\begin{aligned} \tan x &= -i\frac{e^{ix}-e^{-ix}}{e^{ix}+e^{-ix}} = -i + \frac{2i}{1+e^{2ix}} \\ \cot x &= +i\frac{e^{ix}+e^{-ix}}{e^{ix}-e^{-ix}} = +i - \frac{2i}{1-e^{2ix}},\end{aligned} $$ we substitute these into $I$ then apply the geometric series and interchange the order of summation and integration: $$\begin{aligned} I &= 2i\int_{0}^{\pi/4}x\left(\frac{1}{1+e^{2ix}} - \frac{1}{1-e^{2ix}}\right) \, \mathrm dx \\ &= 2i\int_{0}^{\pi/4}x\left(\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(-1)^ne^{2inx} - \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}e^{2inx}\right) \, \mathrm dx \\ &= 2i\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\left((-1)^n-1\right)\int_{0}^{\pi/4}xe^{2inx} \, \mathrm dx. \end{aligned} $$ We handle the inner integral via the substitution $w = 2inx$ then standardly apply integration by parts once: $$\begin{aligned} \int_{0}^{\pi/4}xe^{2inx} \, \mathrm dx &= \frac{1}{(2in)^2}\int_{0}^{i\pi n/2}we^w \, \mathrm dw \\ &= -\frac{1}{4n^2}\left(e^w\left(w-1\right)\right)\Bigg|_{0}^{i\pi n/2} \\ &= e^{i\pi n/2}\left(-\frac{\pi}{8n}i+\frac{1}{4n^2}\right) - \frac{1}{4n^2}. \end{aligned} $$ Substituting this result back into $I$, we have $$I = 2i\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\left((-1)^n-1\right)\left[e^{i\pi n/2}\left(-\frac{\pi}{8n}i+\frac{1}{4n^2}\right) - \frac{1}{4n^2}\right], $$ and since the sum is non-zero only when $n$ is odd we reindex by $n=2k+1$ and simplify to yield $$ I = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\left[(-1)^k\left(-\frac{\pi}{2(2k+1)}i + \frac{1}{(2k+1)^2}\right)+\frac{1}{(2k+1)^2}i\right]. $$ However, since $I$ is real-valued we must have $$ \mathcal{Im}(I) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{1}{(2k+1)^2} - \frac{\pi}{2}\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^k}{2k+1}= 0. $$ The latter sum is simply the Maclaurin series for $\arctan x$ evaluated at $x=1$: $$ \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^k}{2k+1} = \frac{\pi}{4}, $$ and the first sum can be related to the sum in question: $$\begin{aligned} \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{1}{(2k+1)^2} &= \left(\frac{1}{1^2} + \frac{1}{3^2} + \frac{1}{5^2} \cdots\right) \\ &= \left(\frac{1}{1^2} + \frac{1}{2^2} + \frac{1}{3^2} \cdots\right) - \left(\frac{1}{2^2} + \frac{1}{4^2} + \frac{1}{6^2} \cdots\right) \\ &= \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{k^2} - \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{(2k)^2} \\ &= \frac{3}{4}S. \end{aligned} $$ Putting it together, we have $$ \mathcal{Im}(I) = \frac{3}{4}S - \frac{\pi}{2}\cdot \frac{\pi}{4} = 0,$$ and we solve for $S$ to find $$ S = \frac{\pi^2}{6}. $$ We conclude that $$ S = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{k^2} = \frac{\pi^2}{6}. $$
